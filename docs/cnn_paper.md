Optimizing Convolutional Neural Network Loss Functions for Vector Symbolic Architecture Block-Code Representations in Visual Reasoning TasksIntroduction to Neuro-Symbolic Visual Perception and Vector Symbolic ArchitecturesThe integration of deep learning perception models with symbolic reasoning engines represents a highly active frontier in artificial intelligence, specifically engineered to solve complex relational, analogical, and abstract reasoning challenges. Among the most prominent benchmarks for evaluating these capabilities are the Raven's Progressive Matrices (RPM) and its variations, such as the RAVEN and I-RAVEN datasets. In these tasks, an artificial agent is presented with a matrix of visual panels—typically formatted as $80 \times 80$ grayscale images—and must abduce the underlying generative rules governing the sequence to correctly predict the missing panel. Traditional deep learning models, while possessing exceptional perceptual capabilities, frequently fail at the abstract, multi-step analogical reasoning required to generalize across these matrices. Conversely, pure symbolic artificial intelligence systems excel at algorithmic rule application and rigorous logical deduction but are entirely dependent on hand-crafted, noise-free symbolic inputs, rendering them brittle and incapable of directly processing raw visual sensory data.Neuro-symbolic architectures aim to bridge this dichotomy by fusing a neural network perception frontend with a symbolic logic backend. However, this fusion introduces a profound representational bottleneck known as the neural binding problem, which frequently manifests as the "superposition catastrophe". When a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) attempts to process a visual scene containing multiple distinct objects, each possessing independent attributes such as shape, size, color, and position, standard localist neural representations struggle to maintain the discrete boundaries between these objects. The activation patterns blend, creating a loss of structural fidelity where the network can no longer reliably distinguish which specific attribute belongs to which specific object.To circumvent the superposition catastrophe, contemporary neuro-symbolic research has increasingly turned to Vector Symbolic Architectures (VSAs), also known as Hyperdimensional Computing. VSAs operate on the principle that cognitive representations—ranging from atomic concepts to complex, hierarchical data structures—can be encoded as high-dimensional, distributed vectors within a unified geometric space. Through the application of a well-defined algebraic framework comprising binding, bundling (superposition), and permutation operations, VSAs allow for the construction of transparent, manipulable, and mathematically rigorous memory structures. Within a VSA, attributes and objects can be bound together to form structural role-filler representations, and these composite representations can be superposed without losing the ability to reliably extract the original constituent components, provided the dimensionality of the vector space is sufficiently large to exploit the concentration of measure phenomenon.In the specific architectural context under consideration, the VSA utilizes a specialized variant known as a Sparse Block Code (SBC) representation. Rather than utilizing dense bipolar or continuous real-valued hypervectors, the SBC architecture divides a hypervector of total dimension $D$ into $k$ discrete blocks, where each block possesses a length of $l$ dimensions. Given the parameters of $k=8$ blocks and an $l=64$ block length, the total representational dimensionality is $D = 512$. In a strict Sparse Block Code, exactly one element within each of the $k$ blocks is highly active, effectively functioning as a concatenation of $k$ independent one-hot vectors. This structural constraint dramatically reduces the computational overhead and memory footprint of the system while maintaining an immense representational capacity of $64^8$ unique combinatorial states. The CNN's objective is to function as a functional mapping, translating the $80 \times 80$ pixel grid of a RAVEN panel directly into this $k=8, l=64$ VSA block-code space, thereby generating a structured, neuro-symbolic representation of the visual scene that can be processed by the downstream abductive reasoning engine.However, achieving this end-to-end mapping requires defining a differentiable training objective capable of propagating meaningful gradients from the algebraic VSA domain back through the continuous convolutional layers of the CNN. The empirical observation that the current training paradigm—utilizing a block cross-entropy loss against a one-hot Neuro-Vector-Symbolic Architecture (NVSA) role-filler target—fails to converge, stalling at a loss value of approximately $4.16$, represents a fundamental misalignment between the mathematical properties of the VSA binding operation and the optimization dynamics of gradient descent. This report provides an exhaustive diagnostic analysis of this convergence failure and systematically evaluates four advanced architectural and loss-function alternatives: cosine similarity loss applied to superposed codes, Mean Squared Error (MSE) applied to continuous generalized block codes, self-supervised contrastive loss formulations, and decoupled per-attribute classification heads. Through a rigorous theoretical and empirical synthesis, this document establishes the optimal methodologies for training deep neural networks to generate robust VSA representations.Diagnostic Analysis of the Block Cross-Entropy Convergence FailureThe attempt to train a lightweight CNN using a direct block cross-entropy loss against hash-bound, one-hot NVSA role-filler targets has resulted in severe optimization stagnation. The loss curve exhibits a marginal decrease from $4.16$ to $3.99$ over 10 epochs at a relatively high learning rate of $0.05$. This specific trajectory is not indicative of standard neural network phenomena such as vanishing gradients due to excessive depth, nor is it a consequence of an inadequate learning rate. Rather, the value of the loss itself acts as a precise mathematical diagnostic tool, revealing that the CNN is entirely trapped in a state of maximum entropy, effectively outputting a uniform distribution across all output dimensions.The Mathematics of Maximum Entropy in Block CodesIn a neural network trained with a categorical cross-entropy loss function, the objective is to minimize the negative log-likelihood of the target class given the model's predicted probability distribution. For the defined VSA block-code architecture, the output layer of the CNN is logically partitioned into $k=8$ independent blocks, with each block producing a probability distribution over $l=64$ elements via a softmax activation function. The block cross-entropy loss is computed by evaluating the categorical cross-entropy independently for each block and subsequently averaging or summing the results.If the CNN is fundamentally incapable of mapping the input features to the target labels, the optimal strategy for the network—the strategy that minimizes the penalty for being confidently incorrect—is to predict a perfectly uniform distribution across all possible classes. In a block of length $l=64$, a uniform prediction assigns a probability of $p = \frac{1}{64}$ to every element.The categorical cross-entropy loss $L_{CE}$ for a single block, given a one-hot target $y$ where exactly one element is $1$ and all others are $0$, and a uniform prediction $\hat{y}$, is calculated as follows:$$L_{CE} = - \sum_{i=1}^{l} y_i \log(\hat{y}_i)$$$$L_{CE} = - (1) \log\left(\frac{1}{64}\right) = \ln(64)$$The natural logarithm of 64 is approximately $4.15888$. The empirically observed starting loss of $4.16$ aligns perfectly with this mathematical expectation. This confirms that the CNN is not learning a flawed or noisy representation; it is learning absolutely nothing. The network has converged immediately to the maximum entropy prior. The slight drift from $4.16$ to $3.99$ over 10 epochs does not represent a meaningful descent into a functional loss minimum. Instead, it likely reflects the network's optimization algorithm (e.g., Adam or SGD) capturing minor, dataset-specific superficial statistical biases—such as certain one-hot indices occurring fractionally more often than others within the specific training batches—and shifting the uniform distribution slightly to accommodate these global priors. The network is entirely blind to the relationship between the input images and the VSA targets.Topological Destruction via Hash-Based Role-Filler BindingThe root cause of this representational blindness lies in the specific nature of the NVSA role-filler targets, which are described as utilizing "hash-based role-filler binding producing seemingly random one-hot patterns." Understanding why this target encoding destroys the CNN's capacity to learn requires an examination of the interaction between deep learning feature spaces and VSA algebra.In human cognition and symbolic artificial intelligence, knowledge is frequently organized using schemata or frames, which are mental frameworks consisting of abstract "roles" that are occupied by specific "fillers". For example, in a RAVEN panel, a role might be "Object 1 Shape," and the corresponding filler might be "Triangle." The cognitive process of associating these two concepts is known as role-filler binding. Within a Vector Symbolic Architecture, this is achieved mathematically by taking a high-dimensional vector representing the role ($R$) and a separate high-dimensional vector representing the filler ($F$), and combining them using a binding operator ($\otimes$) to produce a new, composite vector ($B = R \otimes F$).In dense VSA implementations, binding is often achieved via operations like circular convolution, while in Sparse Block Codes (SBCs), binding is frequently implemented as a segment-wise circular shift, where the active bits of one vector dictate the permutation or rotation of the blocks in the other vector. Crucially, regardless of the specific mathematical implementation, a rigorous VSA binding operator acts as a cryptographic hash function. The resulting bound vector $B$ is intentionally designed to be orthogonal (or quasi-orthogonal) to both the original role vector $R$ and the original filler vector $F$. Furthermore, if the filler changes even slightly—for instance, changing from "Triangle" to "Square"—the new bound vector will be entirely orthogonal to the previous bound vector.This quasi-orthogonality is highly desirable for symbolic memory storage, but it is fatal for deep learning optimization. Convolutional Neural Networks operate on the continuity principle. The hierarchical layers of convolutional filters extract increasingly complex geometric and textural patterns from the image, creating a continuous latent manifold where visually similar inputs are mapped to geometrically proximate locations in the high-dimensional space. Gradient descent optimization relies on this topological smoothness; it assumes that a small perturbation in the visual input space will result in a proportional, predictable perturbation in the target representation space.By forcing the CNN to directly predict the hash-bound role-filler vectors, the training objective violently breaks this continuity principle. The CNN observes an image of a small triangle and attempts to map it to a specific one-hot pattern. It then observes an image of a medium triangle—a visually proximate input with highly overlapping convolutional feature maps—and is penalized for not outputting an entirely different, seemingly random, orthogonal one-hot pattern. The gradients produced by the block cross-entropy loss become completely decorrelated from the visual feature hierarchy. The loss landscape is transformed into a chaotic, discontinuous surface characterized by pure noise. Confronted with this topological destruction, the gradient descent algorithm cannot find a cohesive direction to update the network weights, causing the network to defensively retreat to the maximum entropy uniform distribution to minimize the catastrophic penalties of incorrect confident guesses.To restore the learning capacity of the CNN, the training paradigm must be restructured to isolate the continuous, similarity-preserving visual perception process from the discontinuous, hash-based algebraic binding process.Framework Alternative I: Additive Cross-Entropy and Bundle-Predictive LearningThe most mathematically sound and empirically validated solution to the topological destruction caused by direct binding is to fundamentally alter the target representation that the CNN is asked to predict. Instead of forcing the CNN to learn the binding operation, the network should be trained to predict an unbound, bundled superposition of the constituent visual attributes. This paradigm, central to the Neuro-Vector-Symbolic Architecture (NVSA) applied to abstract reasoning tasks, utilizes an Additive Cross-Entropy loss function to bridge the continuous and discrete domains.The Principle of Unbound Superposition and BundlingIn VSA algebra, while the binding operator ($\otimes$) destroys similarity to create unique composite structures, the bundling or superposition operator ($\oplus$) explicitly preserves similarity. Bundling is achieved through element-wise addition of vectors, often followed by a thresholding or normalization step to maintain the fixed vector dimensionality and sparsity constraints. If multiple quasi-orthogonal VSA vectors ($a$, $b$, and $c$) are bundled to form a composite vector $S = a \oplus b \oplus c$, the resulting vector $S$ will maintain a high cosine similarity with all of its constituent parts ($a$, $b$, and $c$), while remaining dissimilar to vectors not included in the bundle.The NVSA methodology exploits this similarity-preserving property to create a smooth, continuous target for the CNN. The training architecture begins by defining a frozen, static dictionary codebook matrix, $W$, which contains the randomly initialized, quasi-orthogonal VSA representations for all possible singular, atomic attribute values present in the RAVEN dataset (e.g., discrete vectors for "Square," "Triangle," "Large," "Small," "Black," "White").During the forward pass, an $80 \times 80$ RAVEN panel image is processed by the CNN backbone (frequently a ResNet-18 architecture) to produce a continuous, real-valued query vector $q \in \mathbb{R}^D$, where $D=512$. Crucially, the CNN is not trained to predict a hash-bound target. Instead, the ground truth target for the image panel is constructed by taking the VSA vectors from the fixed dictionary $W$ that correspond to the objects actually present in the image, and bundling them together via element-wise addition.If an image panel $X$ contains $k$ distinct objects corresponding to dictionary indices $\{y_1, y_2, \dots, y_k\}$, the target representation becomes the bundled vector $w_{y_1} \oplus w_{y_2} \dots \oplus w_{y_k}$. Because the CNN is now tasked with predicting a linear superposition of the semantic attributes present in the image, the target space remains topologically continuous with the visual input space. A panel with a small triangle will have a target vector that is geometrically proximate to the target vector for a panel with a large triangle, as both targets will heavily share the underlying structural signature of the "Triangle" dictionary vector. The CNN is freed from the impossible task of learning the cryptographic hash, allowing the convolutional filters to smoothly converge on the visual features.Formulation of the Additive Cross-Entropy LossTo optimize the alignment between the CNN's continuous query vector $q = f_\theta(X)$ and the bundled target representation, a highly specialized loss function is required. Standard block categorical cross-entropy is incompatible with a bundled target, as the target is no longer a strictly one-hot probability distribution, but rather a composite vector occupying a continuous manifold.The NVSA architecture resolves this by employing a novel Additive Cross-Entropy loss function, which evaluates the cosine similarity between the CNN's output query vector and every vector in the frozen dictionary codebook $W$. Due to the similarity-preserving nature of the bundling operation, maximizing the similarity between the query vector and the bundled target vector is mathematically equivalent to simultaneously maximizing the similarity between the query vector and each individual constituent object vector.The Additive Cross-Entropy loss is formalized as follows :$$L(X, Y, \theta) = - \log \frac{\exp(s_l \cdot (\text{sim}(f_\theta(X), w_{y_1}) + \dots + \text{sim}(f_\theta(X), w_{y_k})))}{\sum_{i=1}^m \exp(s_l \cdot \text{sim}(f_\theta(X), w_i))}$$In this formulation:$X$ represents the input $80 \times 80$ RAVEN panel.$Y = \{y_1, \dots, y_k\}$ represents the set of target indices corresponding to the objects present in the panel.$f_\theta(X)$ represents the $512$-dimensional output query vector generated by the CNN parameters $\theta$.$w_{y_i}$ represents the target constituent VSA vectors extracted from the frozen dictionary $W$.$\text{sim}(\cdot, \cdot)$ denotes the cosine similarity metric.$m$ denotes the total number of single-object combinations available in the codebook $W$.$s_l$ is a critical scaling parameter acting as an inverse softmax temperature.The Criticality of Temperature Scaling and Log-Sum-Exp StabilizationThe mathematical mechanics of the Additive Cross-Entropy loss require careful consideration of scale. The cosine similarity metric is strictly bounded within the interval $[-1, +1]$. If these raw cosine similarity values are fed directly into the standard softmax operation embedded within the cross-entropy function, the resulting probability distribution will be highly compressed and insufficiently discriminative. The exponential function within the softmax cannot generate a sharp enough gradient distinction between a correct class with a similarity of $0.8$ and an incorrect class with a similarity of $0.4$.To address this scale sensitivity, the NVSA formulation multiplies the logit vector by the scalar $s_l$, which functions as an inverse temperature parameter. By scaling the logits upward prior to the softmax computation, the differences between highly similar and dissimilar vectors are exponentially magnified. This effectively sharpens the predicted probability mass functions (PMFs), ensuring that the gradients generated during backpropagation heavily penalize the network for assigning similarity mass to incorrect dictionary vectors, thereby driving rapid and decisive convergence.Furthermore, as the network is optimized to pull the query vector toward multiple valid targets simultaneously (the elements of the bundle), the additive nature of the numerator inside the log-sum-exp structure ensures that the network is rewarded for representing all present attributes in superposition, completely avoiding the destructive interference characterized by the superposition catastrophe in standard localist networks.Algorithmic Post-Hoc BindingBy adopting the Additive Cross-Entropy framework, the CNN is optimized purely for feature extraction and similarity mapping. It produces a query vector that accurately reflects the bundled probability mass functions of the attributes present in the image.Once this highly accurate perception phase is complete, the system bridges the gap to the symbolic backend algorithmically. The query vector is subjected to an associative memory search against the dictionary $W$ to cleanly decouple and extract the independent PMFs for shape, size, color, and position. Only after these attributes have been successfully isolated by the neural frontend is the deterministic, hash-based VSA role-filler binding ($\otimes$) applied. Because this binding is executed mathematically in the symbolic backend rather than being learned by the CNN, the system leverages the rigorous cryptographic properties of VSA logic without subjecting the gradient descent optimizer to topological destruction. Empirical evaluations of this paradigm demonstrate that the NVSA frontend, trained with Additive Cross-Entropy, can achieve extraordinary perception accuracies of up to $99.76\%$ on RAVEN constellations, utilizing highly sample-efficient training regimes.Framework Alternative II: Continuous Relaxations and Mean Squared ErrorAn alternative theoretical approach to circumventing the rigidity of hash-bound one-hot targets is to abandon the strict discrete constraints of the $k=8, l=64$ Sparse Block Code and treat the CNN's output space as a continuous regression problem. This paradigm utilizes Mean Squared Error (MSE) to optimize the network against a relaxed, continuous representation known as a Generalized Sparse Block Code (GSBC).Generalized Sparse Block Codes (GSBCs)In a strict Sparse Block Code (SBC), each of the $k$ blocks contains a single binary $1$, with all other elements set to $0$. This extreme sparsity is highly efficient for hardware implementation but imposes harsh penalties during neural network training if the prediction deviates even marginally from the exact target index.Generalized Sparse Block Codes (GSBCs) represent a mathematical relaxation of these constraints. In a GSBC, the elements within each block are no longer restricted to binary values. Instead, they are continuous real numbers, $x \in \mathbb{R}$, subject to specific normalization constraints—typically enforcing that each block maintains a unit $\ell_1$-norm or $\ell_\infty$-norm. Consequently, a GSBC block can be interpreted as a localized Probability Mass Function (PMF), where the continuous values represent the model's probabilistic confidence across the $l=64$ possible states.By targeting a GSBC representation, the CNN's architecture is modified. The final layer applies a bounded activation function—such as a block-wise softmax (to enforce an $\ell_1$-norm) or a sigmoid (to enforce an $\ell_\infty$-norm constraint without strict probability summation)—allowing the network to navigate a continuous 512-dimensional output manifold. This provides the network with the capacity to express perceptual uncertainty; if an object in a RAVEN panel is ambiguous, the network can distribute its prediction mass across multiple related indices within the block, rather than being forced into a binary, high-penalty decision.The Geometric and Optimization Trade-offs of Mean Squared ErrorWhen operating in this continuous GSBC space, one proposed objective function is Mean Squared Error (MSE), which measures the Euclidean distance ($\ell_2$-norm) between the CNN's continuous output vector and the target GSBC vector. While conceptually straightforward, deploying MSE in a high-dimensional, bounded classification space introduces severe geometric optimization liabilities.The fundamental issue arises from the interaction between the MSE loss function and the bounded activation functions (sigmoid or softmax) required to generate the GSBC probabilities. The gradient of an MSE loss function, when computed with respect to the pre-activation logits $z$, inherently contains the derivative of the activation function, $\sigma'(z)$.The mathematical behavior of the sigmoid and softmax derivatives is such that as the model's predictions become highly confident—meaning the logits $z$ become large positive or large negative values—the derivative $\sigma'(z)$ approaches zero. If a CNN making an early, confidently incorrect prediction is evaluated using MSE, the vanishing gradient $\sigma'(z)$ effectively zeroes out the error signal propagating backwards through the network. The model is penalized by the loss function, but receives no actionable gradient to update its weights and correct the error.This phenomenon renders the loss surface of MSE combined with bounded activations strictly non-convex. It creates expansive, multi-dimensional flat plateaus in the loss landscape. Gradient descent algorithms, including momentum-based optimizers like Adam, frequently become irrevocably stalled in these flat regions, leading to unpredictable convergence failures that are highly sensitive to initial random weight initializations.In direct contrast, the categorical cross-entropy loss function is mathematically co-designed with softmax and sigmoid activations. The derivative of the cross-entropy loss with respect to the logits elegantly cancels out the activation derivative term $\sigma'(z)$. The resulting gradient is directly proportional to the raw error $(y - \hat{y})$. This ensures that even highly confident, incorrect predictions generate massive, non-vanishing gradients, forcefully pushing the network away from bad local minima and guaranteeing a strictly convex loss profile.Integration with Block Code Factorizers (BCF)While MSE is generally contraindicated for training the deep convolutional backbone directly against GSBC targets, continuous GSBC representations have proven highly effective when integrated with specialized post-hoc decoding modules. If a CNN is trained (preferably using block-wise cross-entropy) to produce noisy, continuous GSBC product vectors, these vectors must eventually be discretized to support rigorous VSA algebraic reasoning in the symbolic backend.Research by Hersche et al. has demonstrated that this discretization can be elegantly achieved using a Block Code Factorizer (BCF). The BCF is an iterative, in-memory associative search module that can be appended directly after the CNN's convolutional layers, effectively replacing the massive, parameter-heavy Fully Connected Layers (FCLs) typically required for classification.The BCF receives the noisy, continuous GSBC query vector from the CNN and employs a combination of threshold-based nonlinear activations and an $\ell_\infty$-based similarity metric to disentangle the vector back into its constituent, discrete symbolic factors. Because the BCF leverages the computing-in-superposition capabilities of VSAs, it can perform this factorization dynamically without resorting to exhaustive combinatorial searches, typically converging in fewer than five algorithmic iterations.By utilizing a "blockwise additive loss" to train the CNN features to align with the BCF's expectations, this architecture significantly enhances computational efficiency. Studies have shown that deploying a BCF can reduce the total parameter count of deep CNNs by up to $44.5\%$ and lower the computational cost of the classifier layer by up to $86.7\%$, while maintaining a classification accuracy within $4.46\%$ of baseline models using massive fully connected classifiers. Therefore, while MSE is a sub-optimal training objective, continuous GSBC representations hold immense potential when paired with a BCF decoding layer.Framework Alternative III: Self-Supervised Contrastive Loss FormulationsA third paradigm for mitigating the convergence failure shifts the focus away from the specific format of the VSA target and concentrates entirely on optimizing the CNN's feature extraction capabilities. By utilizing Self-Supervised Contrastive Learning (SSCL) formulations, the network can be trained to construct a highly structured latent manifold based purely on the internal visual symmetries and variances of the RAVEN panels, before any explicit mapping to a VSA block-code is attempted.Restructuring the Latent Manifold via InfoNCEContrastive learning frameworks, such as SimCLR, BYOL, and InfoNCE, operate by manipulating the geometric distances between high-dimensional image embeddings in a latent space. The core objective is to pull the embeddings of "positive pairs" (e.g., two augmented views of the exact same image) closer together, while simultaneously pushing the embeddings of "negative pairs" (e.g., embeddings of entirely different images) further apart.In the context of the $80 \times 80$ RAVEN panel images, solving abstract reasoning matrices requires an exceptionally discriminative feature extractor capable of parsing subtle analogical relations—such as tracking the progressive rotation of a triangle or the alternating intensity of a shaded square across the context panels. By applying a contrastive loss to an intermediate dense latent vector (for instance, a $512$-dimensional vector prior to any block-wise reshaping), the CNN is forced to learn these invariant geometric and textural features without the destructive pressure of immediately matching a hash-bound VSA code.The optimization is primarily driven by the InfoNCE (Noise-Contrastive Estimation) loss function, formalized as :$$L_{q, k^+} = -\log \frac{\exp(\text{sim}(q, k^+) / \tau)}{\exp(\text{sim}(q, k^+) / \tau) + \sum_{i=1}^{N} \exp(\text{sim}(q, k_i^-) / \tau)}$$Where:$q$ is the latent representation of the query image.$k^+$ is the representation of the positive key (e.g., the same RAVEN panel subjected to spatial jitter, rotation, or intensity augmentation).$k_i^-$ represents the negative keys drawn from other, distinct panels within the training batch.$\text{sim}(\cdot, \cdot)$ is typically the cosine similarity.$\tau$ is a hyperparameter dictating the temperature of the softmax distribution, controlling the penalty applied to hard negative samples.By minimizing the InfoNCE loss, the CNN backbone establishes a continuous, semantically rich hypersphere. Visual attributes such as object quantity, spatial arrangement, and shape typologies naturally cluster within this space, providing an optimal foundation for subsequent abstract reasoning.Limitations and the Necessity of Multi-Stage ProjectionDespite its profound capacity for feature extraction, pure contrastive learning is structurally insufficient for end-to-end Neuro-Symbolic integration. The fatal limitation of self-supervised contrastive frameworks is that they do not explicitly enforce the discrete, algebraic formatting required by Vector Symbolic Architectures. The latent representations generated by InfoNCE reside on a continuous hypersphere; they do not inherently partition into the sparse $k=8, l=64$ block-code topology, nor do they natively support the rigorous mathematical properties of VSA binding and bundling operators.Consequently, contrastive loss cannot serve as the sole training objective for a VSA perception module. It must be deployed within a multi-stage training pipeline. In phase one, the CNN backbone is pre-trained using the InfoNCE objective on massive volumes of unlabeled RAVEN panels, utilizing large batch sizes to ensure diverse negative mining. This stabilizes the convolutional filters and guarantees spatial awareness. In phase two, a projection head must be appended to the CNN to map the continuous contrastive embeddings into the discrete VSA block-code space. This projection head, and subsequently the entire network, must then be fine-tuned using a structured, supervised loss function—such as the Additive Cross-Entropy loss detailed in Alternative I—to align the learned features with the fixed VSA dictionary codebooks. While computationally intensive, this hybrid approach yields exceptionally robust neuro-symbolic representations.Framework Alternative IV: Decoupled Perception via Per-Attribute Classification HeadsIf the primary engineering objective is to guarantee rapid, fail-safe convergence and bypass the complexities of generating hypervectors entirely within the neural network, the most pragmatic solution is to decouple the deep learning perception apparatus from the VSA algebra. This paradigm, widely utilized in modular neuro-symbolic systems such as the Probabilistic Abduction and Execution (PrAE) model, abandons "end-to-end" hypervector generation in favor of traditional, multi-task deep learning.The Multi-Task Classification ArchitectureIn this decoupled architecture, the CNN is not forced to terminate in a single $512$-dimensional output layer intended to represent the total bound VSA block-code. Instead, the CNN functions as a shared convolutional feature extractor (e.g., a lightweight ResNet or LeNet backbone) that subsequently branches into multiple, independent fully connected classification heads. Each specific head is architected and trained to predict an isolated, discrete semantic attribute of the objects within the RAVEN panel.For a standard visual reasoning dataset, the multi-head structure would be configured as follows:Head 1 (Object Shape): A dense layer outputting a probability distribution over the available shapes (e.g., square, triangle, circle, pentagon).Head 2 (Object Size): A dense layer outputting a distribution over size categories (e.g., small, medium, large).Head 3 (Object Color/Intensity): A dense layer outputting a distribution over the grayscale fill patterns (e.g., solid black, white, striped, dotted).Head 4 (Spatial Position): A dense layer outputting a distribution over the available grid coordinates within the panel.Each of these independent heads is trained using standard Categorical Cross-Entropy against one-hot ground truth labels corresponding to the specific attribute.The Advantage of Convexity and Post-Hoc ConstructionThe overriding advantage of this decoupled approach is the absolute guarantee of optimization stability. Because the targets for each head map directly to isolated, visually observable features, the loss landscape is exceptionally smooth and strictly convex. The CNN is no longer attempting to decode a cryptographic hash or balance continuous superpositions; it is performing basic, multi-class visual recognition. Models employing this isolated, per-attribute methodology routinely achieve near-perfect perception accuracies exceeding $99\%$ on individual RAVEN object attributes, converging rapidly within very few epochs.To integrate this decoupled perception module with the Vector Symbolic Architecture, the VSA block-code must be constructed deterministically and algorithmically outside the neural network. Once the CNN completes its forward pass, the independent classification heads output their respective Probability Mass Functions (PMFs) for shape, size, color, and position. These PMFs are then translated into continuous VSA Generalized Sparse Block Codes by a hard-coded symbolic parser. Finally, the VSA algebraic binding operator ($\otimes$) is executed algorithmically by the CPU/GPU on these translated codes to construct the final composite role-filler representation required by the abductive reasoning engine.The Representational Trade-OffWhile per-attribute classification solves the convergence failure instantly, it comes at a significant theoretical cost. This architecture fundamentally violates the core philosophy of unified neuro-symbolic representation learning. It forces human engineers to explicitly pre-define every semantic head and requires exhaustive, dense metadata labeling for every single attribute within every training image. It inherently limits the system's capacity for unsupervised discovery, preventing the CNN from learning novel, unprogrammed visual relationships that might exist within the data. If the explicit scientific objective of the system is to design a network capable of natively learning structural VSA representations directly from raw pixels, decoupled classification acts as an engineering bypass rather than a fundamental solution to the neural binding problem.Comparative Synthesis and Strategic DirectivesThe empirical observation of the training loss plateauing at approximately $4.16$ provides definitive mathematical proof that the CNN is outputting a uniform distribution across the $l=64$ elements of the $k=8$ blocks. The VSA binding operator structurally destroys the topological continuity required by convolutional filters, rendering gradient descent optimization impossible.To successfully train a lightweight CNN to map $80 \times 80$ RAVEN images into a functional VSA block-code space, the objective function must be fundamentally realigned.
